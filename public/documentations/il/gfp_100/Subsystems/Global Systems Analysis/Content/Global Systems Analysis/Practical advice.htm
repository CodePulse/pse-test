Practical advice

  • It is not advisable to use more than 15 to 20 factors at a time. Consider an example where there is a requirement to generate n points per dimension in an equal gridded fashion, in d dimensions, which requires nd samples. To generate 3 points in 20 dimensions, close to 3.5 billion samples are required. Although the methods used in GSA are very efficient in capturing a picture of the space, dimensionality remains an obstacle.

  • Ensure that the factor space is appropriately defined. Consider an example where only two phase behaviour is of interest, and suppose that both temperature and pressure are specified as factors. If the factor space were defined independently, a square space would be obtained which would include single phase behaviour in the results.

  • Group sampling techniques should be employed for very large sets of factors. This technique is best explained via a simple example. Consider an example with 10 factors, x1 to x10, to which new factors , w1 and w2, are introduced. If the equations are added:

  • Do not focus on a single metric or figure when analysing results. It is clear from the definition of the metrics that none of them can be interpreted in isolation. Moreover, figures typically result from the projection of a larger dimensional space onto a one- (histogram) or two- (2d scatter plot) dimensional space. Therefore, combining metrics and figures provides more reliable insight.
