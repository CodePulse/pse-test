The intestinal membrane contains plicate and villi which increase the surface area available for permeation. The plicate become smaller and less numerous as the distance along the small intestine increases. The plicate expansion factors were determined by linearly decreasing the value from 3 to 1 based on the intestinal segment length. The villi expansion factors for each intestinal segment were determined from the ratio of the smooth tube surface areas of each segment to the effective surface area (i.e. accounting for the plicate and villi) based on the work of Willmann et al. (2004) 2Willmann, S., Schmitt, W., Keldenich, J., Lippert, J. and Dressman, J.B., 2004. A physiological model for the estimation of the fraction dose absorbed in humans. Journal of medicinal chemistry, 47(16), pp.4022-4031.. It is assumed a single villus can be regarded as a cylindrical tube with a spherical top. The average height of the villi is reported to decrease linearly with increasing distance from 800 µm (proximal end) to 500 µm (distal end of the small intestine). The radius of a single villus is nearly constant within the small intestine (approximately 50 µm), and their reported density is approximately 25 per mm2. For the amplification factor of the microvilli, a mean value of 25 was used (the reported individual variability is between 15 and 40). In order to permeate, species must move from the lumen through an unstirred water layer covering the intestinal membrane to the membrane surface. Various permeation routes are then available (e.g. transcellular, active transport etc.), including paracellular permeation through the cell wall tight junctions.
