Dissolution and growth

For each solid phase, a notional liquid surface adjacent to all solid particles is considered. It is assumed that:

  1. As the surface has zero volume, there is no accumulation of material.
  2. The dissolution process results in solid species being added to this surface at a rate of (mol m-2 s-1).
  3. Instantaneous interconversion between species, taking place at a rate of (mol m-2 s-1), has to take place on this surface in order to preserve chemical reaction equilibrium.
  4. The rate-limiting step of the dissolution process is the mass transfer from the solid surface to the bulk liquid via a laminar sub-layer known as the unstirred water layer.
  5. All liquid species diffuse through the unstirred water layer with unique diffusivities

The flux of diffusion of each species from each particle size bin is linked to the bulk liquid phase mass balance by converting the molar flux to a mass change rate. This is done by converting the molar flux to the mass flux, then multiplying by the total surface area of particles. The total surface area of particles in each bin is calculated by multiplying the number of particles in the bin by the surface area of one particle. The flux of diffusion of each species from each particle size bin is linked to the population balance by converting the molar flux to a linear growth/dissolution rate, accounting for the particle shape.
