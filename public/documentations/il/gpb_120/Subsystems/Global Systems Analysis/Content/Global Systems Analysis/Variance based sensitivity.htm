Variance-based sensitivity method

The variance-based method is the most comprehensive sensitivity method available in gPROMS. It determines the contribution of each factor to the variance in a response.

The metrics used are based on variances and are indicative of what part of the variance, in the response, is attributed to the variance in each factor. Note that the relative contribution of the factors to the variance in the response can be sorted differently for each response.

Both metrics are relative, that is, normalised by the total variance in the response. Consequently, first order effect indices are always smaller than or equal to one, because the influence from other factors is not considered. In contrast, the total effect is always larger than or equal to one, due to the combined effects of the factors.

Variance-based sensitivity analysis has different applications, for example:

  • Factor prioritisation
    • This is used to identify factors which, when fixed to their actual value, reduce the variance in the response the most. Factor prioritisation allows the user to:
      • Identify and sort the factors which need to be more accurately measured in order to reduce the response variance
      • Identify the factors to be estimated in a subsequent numerical or experimental estimation process.
    • Saltelli and Tarantola (2002) have demonstrated that the first order effect index facilitates factor prioritisation. Note that the first order effect index is less accurate than the total effect index, and that any negative values reported should be disregarded, although the total effect index is typically still correct.
  • Factor fixing
    • This is used to identify factors which, if left to vary over their range of uncertainty, make no significant contribution towards the variance of the response. These factors can be fixed at any point within their range of variation, without affecting the response variance. Detecting and fixing non-influential factors can significantly simplify the system.
    • Saltelli and Tarantola (2002) also demonstrated that the total effect index facilitates factor fixing. Note that a null total effect index is a necessary and sufficient condition for a factor to be non-influential.

The main shortcoming of the variance-based sensitivity analysis is its high computational cost, which becomes prohibitive for computationally expensive models. Estimating the sensitivity indices requires a large number of model evaluations. In fact, N*(k+2) model evaluations are required for each deterministic scenario, where N is the number of probabilistic samples requested and k the number of probabilistic factors. For example, for a model with 15 probabilistic factors, at least 17000 simulations will be executed per scenario when N=1000.

Screening methods will be available in future versions as viable alternatives for computationally expensive models.

Illustrative example of a variance-based sensitivity analysis

To better understand the model, it is beneficial to compare the relative impact of all factors which influence the KPIs in the production of methyl acetate in the CSTR. A variance-based sensitivity analysis is computationally more demanding, but more accurate than the elementary effects method, enabling a comparison of the relative influences of the factors and factor combinations, rather than only identifying the main factors.

Consider factors coolant temperature, inlet molar flow rate, inlet molar fraction of methanol, inlet temperature and pressure. The responses of interest are the yield, temperature, and molar concentrations of methyl acetate and acetic anhydride. Variance-based sensitivity analysis indicates the variance in the yield is mostly caused by the inlet molar fraction of methanol.
