Bed loading definitions

The packed bed contains an amount of adsorbent in the form of pellets containing active material. The following definitions describe the amount of active adsorbent material in the bed as well as the space in the bed occupied by the adsorbent pellets .

Bed bulk density. This is the amount of adsorbent material per volume of bed. kgadsorbent/mbed3
Adsorbent pellet bulk density. This is amount of adsorbent material per unit volume. This density includes the intra-particle volume. kgadsorbent/madsorbent3
Bed void fraction (or "Bed porosity"). This is the amount of void space in the bed between the particles per unit volume of bed. mvoid,extraparticle3/mbed3
Void fraction of the adsorbent pellets. This is the amount of intraparticle void space in the adsorbent pellets. mvoid,intraparticle3/madsorbent3
Total mass of adsorbent in the bed or per layer. kgadsorbent
Adsorbent material density. This is the material density of the adsorbent material. It excludes the intra-particle pore volume. kg/m3

Total void fraction. This is the total void space (intraparticle and between the particles) per unit volume of bed.

Bed header models

At the feed and product ends of the bed, there are generally inert sections. These sections are modelled using lumped gas holdups of a specified volume (usually the volume is a small percentage of the bed volume). The key equations for these models are identical to the ones used for the Junction reversible model ('Junction')

Linear Driving Force (LDF) model

To predict the mass transfer from the fluid phase to the adsorbed phase, a Linear Driving Force (LDF) model is used. This means that the molar flux from the fluid to the surface is a linear function of the concentration differences between the fluid phase concentration and the equilibrium concentration as predicted by the isotherm for a given surface coverage. This driving force can be calculated on different bases:

  • Difference between the actual surface molar concentration and the equilibrium surface molar concentration as predicted by the isotherm
  • Difference between the actual fluid phase concentration and the equilibrium fluid phase concentration as predicted by the isotherm
  • Difference between the fluid phase partial pressure and the equilibrium fluid phase partial pressure as predicted by the isotherm

Isotherms

The isotherms are used by the model to predict the amount of material that is absorbed for each component, when the gas phase and the surface are in equilibrium. There are three default options and a custom modelling option available to specify the adsorption isotherms for a multicomponent mixture:

  • Langmuir
  • Langmuir-Freundlich
  • Ideal Adsorbed Solution (IAS) theory
  • Custom isotherm
