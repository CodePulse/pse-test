Permeation

The following rate expression is used to calculate the volume specific rate of permeation:

The surface area available for permeation is calculated from the fluid volume in each intestinal segment. The intestinal segment radius and length is fixed, and it is assumed that the fluid is always in full contact with the radial dimension of the intestinal surface. Therefore, when the fluid volume changes as material is transited in and out of the intestinal segment, the axial dimension of segment in contact with the fluid will change, resulting in an increase or decrease in the wetted surface area. The alternative approach to calculate the wetted surface area (not currently implemented) is to assume that the fluid in the intestinal segment is distributed axially, and therefore as the fluid volume changes, contact with the radial dimension will change. As easy way to think of this is to consider a partially filled pipe containing water. If the pipe is vertical, as you fill the pipe the axial dimension of the fluid pocket increase. If the pipe is horizontal, the radial dimension would increase.

The system is considered to be well mixed and the concentration of drug on the other side of the membrane is considered to be 0. No permeation occurs in the stomach.
