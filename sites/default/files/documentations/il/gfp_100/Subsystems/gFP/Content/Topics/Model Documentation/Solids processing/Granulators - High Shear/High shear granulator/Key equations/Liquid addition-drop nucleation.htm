Liquid addition, drop nucleation, and wetting

When liquid is added to the granulator, some of the liquid participates in nucleation, while the remainder wets the surfaces of nucleated granules. The fraction of liquid that contributes to nucleation is equal to the mass fraction of ungranulated fine powder in the system.

The drop nucleation model is based on the work of Barrasso and Ramachandran (2015)1Barrasso, D. and Ramachandran, R. Qualitative assessment of a multi-scale, compartmental PBM-DEM model of a continuous twin-screw wet granulation process. Journal of Pharmaceutical Innovation, 2015, 1-19.. Droplets are given a lognormal size distribution with a user-specified mean and standard deviation. The droplets penetrate a bed of fine powder particles, replacing inter-particle void. In the newly-formed granule, the volumetric ratio of liquid to total pore space is equal to the specified maximum pore saturation fraction. The nucleated granule size is determined from the droplet size, the bed porosity, and the maximum pore saturation fraction. The birth of granules due to drop nucleation results in the depletion in fine powder. It is assumed that the droplet size of the liquid sprayed onto the powder bed is significantly larger than the fine powder particles.

The remaining liquid contributes to wetting. The wetting of particles is assumed to happen instantaneously. Wetting is evaluated as a growth term in the granulate population balance in which the total mass growth rate over all granules is equal to the liquid addition rate excluding that contributing to nucleation. A size-independent kinetic model is available such that the linear growth rate due to wetting is independent of granule size.
