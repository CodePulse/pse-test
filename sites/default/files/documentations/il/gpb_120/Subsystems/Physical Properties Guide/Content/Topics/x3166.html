Electrolytic physical property methods

The gPROMS Electrolytic Physical Properties interface specifies that all electrolytic physical property packages interfaced to gPROMS need to provide the Thermophysical property methods and their arguments listed in the first table below.

The form of the methods described here has been chosen so as to maximise the reliability and accuracy of any gPROMS within which they are incorporated. This is particularly important in view of the extremely low concentrations of some species in many electrolytic applications. We recommend that you make use of these methods in any models that you develop1The details of how precisely to refer to Foreign Object methods in your Models are described in: Using Foreign Objects in gPROMS Models and Incorporating Physical Properties in Models. In this manner, you will also ensure that your models will be usable with any electrolytic physical property package interfaced to gPROMS.

The following points should be noted about the tables below:

  1. The inputs of the methods in the first table are as follows:

    • T : temperature (K)

    • P : pressure (Pa)

    • nx : natural logarithm of the amounts of each species in the liquid phase (in mol)

    • ny : natural logarithm of the amounts of each species in the vapour phase (in mol)

    • ns : natural logarithm of the amounts of each species in the solid phase (in mol)

    • nz : natural logarithm of the amounts of each molecular species (in mol)

  2. Names of components and equilibrium constants:

    The names of components in the various phases and also of equilibrium constants can be retrieved by dedicated methods.

    1. Names of components cover the following sets:

      • vapour species

      • liquid species

      • solid species

      • molecular species

      • true species.

    2. Names of equilibrium constants regard the following equilibria:

      • vapor-liquid phase

      • liquid-phase reactions

      • liquid-solid phase.

  3. The following methods return the natural logarithms of the corresponding quantities:

    • VapourFugacity :

    • LiquidActivity :

    • VapourFugacityCoefficients :

    • LiquidActivityCoefficients :

    • VapourLiquidEquilibriumConstants :

    • LiquidReactionEquilibriumConstants :

    • SolidDissociationEquilibriumConstants :

  4. The datum for all enthalpy calculations is the elemental species at standard conditions. One implication of this is that "heat generation'' terms must not be included in energy conservation equations to account for chemical reactions.

  5. The results of the three equilibrium flash methods (MolecularTPEquilibrium, MolecularTPEquilibriumExt and TrueSpeciesTPEquilibrium) are organised as detailed in the third and fourth table. The quantities returned in the results vector of the methods in both tables include the natural logarithms of molar fractions and amounts.

  6. For convenience in the construction of some complex models, three additional quantities are defined:

    • Vapour Liquid Sum (VLS)

      Thus, the VLS is the logarithm of the ratio of the right hand side of equation

      to its left hand side. Under conditions of vapour/liquid equilibrium, its value should be zero.

    • Liquid Reaction Sum (LRS)

      Thus, the LRS is the logarithm of the ratio of the right hand side of equation

      to its left hand side. Under conditions of reaction equilibrium, its value should be zero.

    • Solid Dissociation Sum (SDS)

      Thus, the SDS is the logarithm of the ratio of the right hand side of equation

      to its left hand side. If the solid species j is in equilibrium with the liquid phase, its value should be zero, but this holds true only for the solids that actually form. Those solids j for which the activities of the forming ions are smaller than the corresponding equilibrium constant Kjsr will not form at all and the equilibrium relationsship cannot be applied.

    The three above quantities are returned by methods VapourLiquidSum, LiquidReactionSum and SolidDissociationSum respectively.
