Influence of factor space shape

Typically, the shape of the factor space is only of interest for uncertainty and sensitivity analyses, and not for scenario or parametric analysis.

The shape and size of the factor space from which samples are drawn is determined by:

  • The lower and upper bounds of each factor
  • The type of distribution of each factor
  • Correlations between factors
  • Sampling restrictions.

For example:

  • Uniform distributions for factors with no correlations or sampling restrictions return hyper-cube shapes, that is, a square for two dimensions, a cube for three dimensions, and so forth.
  • Normal distributions for factors with no correlations or sampling restrictions return hyper-sphere shapes, that is, a circle for two dimensions, a sphere for three dimensions, and so forth.

Although these attributes are easy to specify, other shapes may be more appropriate, depending on the circumstances. For example, consider the flash separation of a mixture of pentane and heptane. A typical scenario would be where there is both vapour and liquid in the system. Using a uniform distribution for both temperature and pressure, it is possible to sample from the rectangle outlined in the figure below whereas, ideally, sampling should only be from within the ellipsoid.

Rectangular and ellipsoidal factor spaces for flash separation example

Specify the objective function as a response and define it as the amount of valuable product coming out of the flash, that is, the sum of the mole fraction of hexane multiplied by the output vapour flow rate plus the mole fraction of heptane multiplied by the output liquid flow rate. Since the inlet flow rate is 2 kg/s and the mass composition is 50% hexane, the flash separation only produces both vapour and liquid streams when the value of the response is larger than one.
